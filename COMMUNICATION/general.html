OCR

The original image on the left is just a picture. It's not actually words, it's just pictures of words.

Adobe Acrobat Pro DC has the ability to use Optical Character Recognition (OCR) to turn images into real words that screen-readers can grab. Screen Readers aren't able to make this happen until you OCR the document.

The video below shows what happens when a totally inaccessible image is run through Adobe Acrobat Pro DC's OCR process. To me, it's an astounding transformation from something totally inaccessible to something that may be heard by a blind man.

The Current State, according to Open News.org

The current slate of good document recognition OCR engines use a mix of techniques to read text from images, but they are all optimized for documents. They assume that material fits on a rectangular page. Most start with a line detection process that identifies lines of text in a document and then breaks them down into words or letter forms. Some use a dictionary to improve results—when a string is ambiguous, the engine will err on the side of the known word. A dictionary isn’t always enough, however, as Wesley Raabe learned as he was transcribing the 1879 edition of Uncle Tom’s Cabin.

Captioning

Although captioning can be done by hand, there are many automated systems coming down the pipe that will make life easier. For academic purposes, the results still need to be reviewed by a human hand, since the captioning needs to be near 100% accuracy. But automated systems can boost productivity significantly.
